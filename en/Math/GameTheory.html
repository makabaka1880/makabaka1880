Probability Space

A probability space is a mathematical construct that formalizes the concept of a random experiment. It consists of three elements:

  1. Sample Space (SSS): This is the set of all possible outcomes of the random experiment. For example, if you're rolling a fair six-sided die, the sample space would be S={1,2,3,4,5,6}S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}S={1,2,3,4,5,6}.

  2. Event Space (F\mathcal{F}F): This is a collection of subsets of the sample space SSS, called events, such that certain rules are satisfied. These rules usually include that the sample space SSS and the empty set ∅\emptyset∅ are in F\mathcal{F}F, and that the event space is closed under complements (if AAA is in F\mathcal{F}F, then so is its complement) and countable unions (the union of countably many events in F\mathcal{F}F is also in F\mathcal{F}F).

  3. Probability Measure (PPP): This is a function that assigns a probability to each event in the event space. The probability measure PPP satisfies certain properties, such as:

    • Non-negativity: P(A)≥0P(A) \geq 0P(A)≥0 for all events AAA.
    • Normalization: P(S)=1P(S) = 1P(S)=1, indicating that the probability of the entire sample space is 1.
    • Countable additivity: If A1,A2,…A_1, A_2, \ldotsA1​,A2​,… are pairwise disjoint events (i.e., they have no outcomes in common), then P(⋃i=1∞Ai)=∑i=1∞P(Ai)P(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i)P(⋃i=1∞​Ai​)=∑i=1∞​P(Ai​).

The Prisoner's Dilemma

A very interesting and classical example.

Scenario

Two suspects in a crime are put into separate cells. If they both confess, each will be sentenced to three years in prison. If only one of them confesses, he will be freed and used as a witness against the other, who will receive a sentence of four years. If neither confesses, they will both be convicted of a minor offense and spend one year in prison.

It's pretty easy to construct the consequence map. We can directly use the length of the sentence for the map, and it's still a nice A→NA\to \mathbb NA→N map, only now the player wants fff to be as low as possible.

It's pretty obvious that for any player in this game, they could reach a fff of 0 when they confess. Because in a Nash equilibrium, players don't care about each other, so they both tend to confess. This give this game the unique equilibrium (Confess,Confess)(Confess, Confess)(Confess,Confess).

Warning

Note that in a Nash equilibrium, we don't care about ∑f\sum f∑f. It's only when there are multiple equilibria we consider other factors such as the common profit in a game. This is why the prisoners don't both choose not to confess even though it gives the best result for both of them.

2.5 Strictly Competitive Games

While it is pretty hard to find Nash equilibria of an arbitrary strategic games, there are a few exceptions.

Definition 5.0

A strategic game ⟨{1,2},(Ai),(≿i)⟩\langle\{1, 2\}, (A_i), (\succsim_i)\rangle⟨{1,2},(Ai​),(≿i​)⟩ is strictly competitive when ∀a,b∈A\forall a, b \in A∀a,b∈A we have a≿1ba \succsim_1 ba≿1​b if and only if a≾2ba \precsim_2 ba≾2​b. This just means that for two players competing, an action aaa is only better then bbb for player 1 when it is worse for player 2. Strictly competitive games are also called zerosums because the payoff function uiu_iui​ for the two players add up to u1+u2=0u_1 + u_2 = 0u1​+u2​=0.

When player iii maxminimizes his actions, that means that it is best for him on the assumption that whatevery he does, his opponent jjj will try her best to hurt him as much as possible. This can only happen under a strictly competitive game. In a sense, the maximinimizer is the best-response function in a strictly competitive game.

Now we want to show that in a strictly competitive game, the tuples of maxminimizers for every player is the Nash equilibria.

This basically means maximizing u(x∗,y)u(x^*, y)u(x∗,y) for player 1, and making it at least as good as any other u(x,y)u(x, y)u(x,y).
Here, operater min⁡\minmin means finding the smallest value for the expression the operator is acting on, while maintaining the expression in the subscript true. Ex, min⁡x≥3,x∈Nx2≤10\min_{x \geq 3, x \in \mathbb N}{x^2} \leq 10minx≥3,x∈N​x2≤10, then x=3x = 3x=3.
